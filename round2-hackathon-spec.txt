

We’re excited to share the core problem themes for Round 2 - the foundation your solutions will be built on.

These include:

Multi-Agent Interactions
Long-Horizon Planning & Instruction Following
World Modeling across professional and personal tasks
Self-Improving agent systems

These themes reflect the direction of real-world AI environments and agent behavior that this hackathon is designed to evaluate.

Your task is to choose any one (or more) of these themes and design your own problem statement around it. The goal is to simulate realistic scenarios, enable meaningful agent interaction, and support measurable outcomes.

As part of your submission, you are expected to clearly define:

The **problem statement**
The **environment** in which the agent(s) will operate
The **capabilities** of the agent(s)
The **tasks** to be performed
The **reward model/evaluation logic**
The **post-training or self-improvement strategy**

We've also included the detailed judging criteria for this round in the attached document here: Read Here

We strongly recommend reviewing it carefully- high-scoring submissions will not just match a theme, but will also:

Define clear, structured tasks and environments
Incorporate robust evaluation and reward mechanisms
Reflect real-world complexity aligned with OpenEnv principles

You can begin working on your problem statement right away. The training and implementation phase will take place during the on-site hackathon, where you will be provided with compute credits.

Use this time to thoughtfully refine both the design and evaluation aspects of your solution.

What the automated round checks
These are the items the validation pass looks for. If any is missing or broken at the deadline, the submission won't make it to a human judge; regardless of how strong the underlying idea is. Verify each one explicitly before you submit.

Public, cloneable Hugging Face Space at the submitted URL. Test from a logged-out browser. Private spaces, dead links, or 404s are an automatic out.
Valid OpenEnv structure: proper Environment / MCPEnvironment base class, Gym-style reset / step / state, and a parseable openenv.yaml.
Training evidence committed to the repo as image files (.png / .jpg): At minimum a loss curve and a reward curve. Wandb-only links and plots that live only in a Colab cell don't count: they may not be reachable when validation runs.
A runnable training script (Unsloth, HF TRL, or other frameworks), preferably linked as a Colab notebook so it can be re-executed end to end (Python script is acceptable as well).
A README that links every deliverable: HF Space, training notebook, and your writeup (blog / video / slides), with the key plots embedded inline. If validation can't reach a deliverable from the README, it counts as missing.

Few tips on Hugging Face Blog posts and Colab links,

For writing Hugging Face blog posts, you can write a markdown article and place it in your environment code repository.
For training run notebook, please ensure that is runnable and doesn't contain errors. You can either share the codebase link to training script's ipynb or add the publicly accessible Google Colab notebook link to your README.
